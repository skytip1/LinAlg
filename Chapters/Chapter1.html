Matrices and gaussian Elimination

Before we start talking about Matrices and Gaussian Elimination you should be comfortable with the notion of vectors. The books first Chapter skims Over the concept of vectors pretty fast but you should really take a minute to know what a vecor represents. Below I have attatched some videos showing what a vector is.

Now that you have a solid grasph on what vectors are we should get into the algebra of it.

There are some simple opperations we can do with vectors. For instance take the vector $$\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}$$ we can multiply any number to this vector and this gives the effect of "scailing" the vecor geometrically or in other words, $$c \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} cv_1 \\ cv_2 \\ cv_3 \end{bmatrix} = c\begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Here is a more interactive way you can view scailing a vector.

Now one vector is cool but what's even cooler is multiple vectors. This is where the beauty of Linear alegbra shines because we can represent as many vectors as we want, even an infinite amount!

Now if you play around long enough you may notice some things. (I highly reccomend to play around and write down what you notice) You may notice that some of these can lines interesect at a point, while others (the green and blue) never intersect. And you may also notice that at some values none of the lines intersect. So what did I graph, what do these represent and why is intersection so important?

The vectors or (linear equations) I graphed were the following $$-ax-by=-\frac{1}{2}$$ $$ax-by=\frac{1}{9}$$ $$2ax-3by=\frac{1}{6}$$ $$ax\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} +by\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ -1 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} -1/2 \\ 1/9 \\ 1/6 \end{bmatrix}$$ You may notice these are just equations of a line! This is where the name "Linear Algebra" comes from as we are just manipulating a bunch of linear equations.
